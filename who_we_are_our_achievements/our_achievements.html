    Significant Achievements and Impacts

    The projects of the Arannayk Foundation have made significant impact toward restoration and conservation of biodiversity in national Protected Areas (PA) as well as in community conserved forests and rural homesteads. Some specific examples of such impacts are:

    Forest Conservation through promotion of alternative livelihood

    The Arannayk Foundation adopted a strategy of forest conservation through improving livelihood of forest dependent communities. Forest dependent communities were organized for improved management of their organizations and helped to improve their technical skill for alternative income generating activities (AIGA). To provide access to fund, they were encouraged to start their own saving program followed by project grant to develop a revolving loan fund. In almost all projects of Arannayk Foundation such provisions are made. Now there are 558 CBOs supported under 25 projects and the amount of grants provided by the Arannayk Foundation as RLF grant is BDT 24.3 million. On the contrary, the amount of savings by the CBO is around 14.8 million. The CBO members are using the money for alternative income generating activities either individually or in group (eg. In case of community patrol groups, they are using the money for large scale investment in group). The RLF fund is managed by the communities. This fund is used for providing loan to the CBO members. Each individual member can have a loan of up to BDT 25,000 for one year for any alternative income generating activities. The loan is either interest free or at a minimum rate of interest as set by the CBOs to cover the cost of communication and entertainment during monthly meeting. So far, the CBOs have adopted more than 30 AIGAs. The profitability varies with locality and market demand. However, in general, households look for a quick return from AIGA. In most cases, vegetable cultivation is found to be the first choice. Other profitable AIGAs include cattle fattening, cow rearing, fish farming, small trade, rickshaw and van buying, tailoring, land lease in for agriculture and handicrafts making.

    Collaborative Forest Management:

    Jointly with Forest Department, Arannayk Foundation has contributed to conserve Inani Forest, Chunati Wildlife Sanctuary, Banshkhali Forest, Sitakunda-Mirsarai forests, Singra National Park and Dhamoirhat, Altadighi National Park, covering a total area of about 25,000 hectares. Those forests have been brought under co-management system involving local communities and community patrol groups. Illicit extraction of timber, fuel wood and bamboos; poaching of wildlife and incidence of forest fire have significantly reduced in these forests due to increased awareness among local communities, better collaboration between local communities and Forest Department, forest protection by community patrol groups (CPG) and adoption of alternative income generating activities (AIGA) by forest dependent people using the revolving loan fund (RLF) support of the AF projects. It may be mentioned that in Inani (10,000 ha) forest, extraction of fuel wood from the forest by local communities (annual) reduced from 60,000 tons in 2011 to 50,000 tons in 2013, round timber from 506,000 cft in 2011 to 303,000 cft in 2013, sawn timber from 4,438,000 cft in 2011 to 798,000 cft in 2013, poles from 1,351,000 pieces in 2011 to 2,380,000 pieces in 2013 and bamboo from 1,130,000 pieces in 2011 to 169,000 pieces in 2013.

    Restoration of degraded community conserved areas in CHT:

    In Chittagong Hill Tracts, 14 degraded Village Common Forests (VCF) with a total area of 878 ha and 1,110 ha of denuded un-classed state forests (USF) in CHT have been brought under restoration and sustainable management through community mobilization along with their technical, organizational and management skills development, alternative livelihood development support and planting of native tree species in the degraded areas of the forests including bamboos along stream banks. As a result, vegetation cover of the forests and the flow of water in the streams in dry season have increased significantly.

    Restoration of degraded swamp forest

    In the haor (basin land) area of Sunamganj, six highly degraded fresh water swamp forests (168 ha) have been successfully restored by the project participants by leasing the forest land for ten years from the Government. In another ‘haor’ area in Netrokona district, the project participants have established 6.5 km of strip-plantation of hijal (Barringtonia acutangula) and koroch (Pongamia pinnata) trees in erosion-vulnerable areas of four villages.

    Nursery program for promotion of endangered species plantation

    Through supporting 26 local nurseries throughout Bangladesh for raising endangered species, 150 species have been identified, of which 50 species were identified as critically endangered. Nursery entrepreneurs were trained and supported on raising seedlings of critically endangered species. Those nurseries are now producing seedlings of endangered species along with other species making indigenous species available for plantation. After Rohingya Influx in 2017, the Arannayk Foundation has been working in the restoration of degraded forests within camps as well as in the neighboring areas and Sheikh Jamal Inani National Parks. One of the emphases of the Arannayk Foundation has been to make sure that all agencies involved in tree plantations use the quality planting materials of forest trees, fruit trees, bamboo and other species. The AF supported 65 nurseries of Cox’s Bazar district through training them on production of local species in their nurseries. The Arannayk Foundation developed a nursery quality audit system and guideline, which is now used by the nurseries in Cox’s Bazar

    Forest Resources Inventory

    The Arannayk Foundation has been using forest resource collection inventory as one of the important tools for measuring impact of project interventions on forest conservation. In co-management projects, resources collection inventory was made twice in a month: one on market day and the other on non-market day at the entry point to forests. In each entry point, two enumerators use to track entry of people in forests in the morning and their head loads on their return at the end of the day. The collected forest products were then monitized following market price. Thus, the weight and value of the products were calculated and changes in trends determined. One of the main objectives of the study was to measure tangible ecosystem service benefit, particularly the amount of forest resources collected by the forest dependent communities during different months of the year. Resource inventory was conducted in six major forests such as Inani, Chunati, Sitakund-Mirsarai, Singra and coastal forests. The study revealed that with improved livelihood, collection of forest resources reduced resulting in increased natural regeneration of indigenous species. The Arannayk Foundation developed a guideline for forest resource collection inventory and used by other development partners as well.

    Socio-economic and bio-physical Survey

    The Arannayk Foundation in collaboration with FAO developed tools for socio-economic survey for the last Bangladesh Forest Inventory of Bangladesh Forest Department. In addition, the Arannayk Foundation uses socio-economic survey for all its projects where communities are involved. To identify forest dependent households, socio-economic and bio-physical survey is carried out. The AF has been using Tab-Based survey for household profiling since 2018. Socio-economic and bio-physical surveys are conducted at the beginning and at the end of the project to measure the livelihood improvement and changes in biodiversity of the target areas. In addition, GPS survey is used for household location, various important locations and training and validation of data collection for land use land cover mapping. GIS/RS analysis are done to prepare land use land and cover maps and detect changes over time for different forest areas.

    Value-chain studies of different forest products and agricultural commodities

    The Arannayk Foundation has conducted value chain studies of potential commodities in Chunati to support livelihood of forest dependent communities. Similar studies are also conducted for livelihood development component of ‘Climate Resilient Participatory Afforestation and Reforestation Project’ funded by BCCRF and managed by the World Bank. In addition, under USAID supported GREEN LIFE project also value chain was studied and based on the study results, the market linkages were established.

    Database development and management

    The Arannayk Foundation has developed a database of wildlife present in different protected areas which is posted on the website of the Arannayk Foundation. In addition, it has developed a database of 65 nurseries of Cox’s Bazar. It provides information about availability of species with their numbers in each nursery.

    Enrichment of homestead biodiversity

    Through two dedicated projects (during 2006-2009), about 50 species of endangered native trees have been restored in about 4,000 homesteads including 1,000 homesteads in a coastal area in the south-west region and 3,000 households in three non-coastal areas (one in south-west and two in south-east region) of the. In those 4000 homesteads, 400,000 trees of 50 species have been established. Subsequently, through more than 20 other projects of the Arannayk Foundation, more than 15,000 households have planted over a million trees (diverse species) in their homesteads.

    Conservation of medicinal plants

    More than 150 species of local medicinal plants of Chittagong Hill Tracts (CHT) have been conserved in several places of Rangamati district (CHT) and 25 species of medicinal plants of the plain land have been conserved in 10 institutional (school) premises and 400 private homesteads in Keshabpur in Jessore district in the south-west of Bangladesh. Aside from saving them from probable extinction, the medicinal plants, so conserved in two project areas, will help in primary health care of local communities and also in further multiplication and distribution in other areas. There has been an increasing industrial and commercial demand of some of the medicinal plants.

    Capacity building on adaptation to changes including climate change

    Arannayk Foundation, with support from ICIMOD, raised awareness and understanding of stakeholders of Chittagong Hill Tracts on adaptation to changes including climate change. It organized training for different stakeholders such as officers from government organizations, school and college teachers, journalists, NGO staff and research organizations. Among the participants 33% were female. The key issues identified for adaptation to changes including climate change were conservation and restoration of natural forests for biodiversity, sustained supply of water and need for promoting alternative livelihood. A massive mass awareness program is needed to stop conversion of natural forests for non-forest uses.

    Restoration of critically endangered species

    63 endangered native tree species of Bangladesh have been saved from local extinction through establishment of conservation plots (also serving as seed stands) within Chittagong University Campus and multiplication of those species by 27 private nurseries in different parts of Bangladesh.

    Promotion of improved cooking stoves (ICS)

    About 10,600 forest dependent households have adopted improved cook-stoves (ICS). The use of ICS reduced household consumption of biomass fuel by about 50%. With traditional cook-stoves, a rural household in Bangladesh consumes 90 kg fuel wood per month on the average. Thus, the ICSs adopted by the beneficiaries of Arannayk projects save an estimated 5,724 tons of fuel wood and 10,493 tons of CO2 emission per year.

    Establishment of revolving loan fund

    The most notable impact of the Arannayk Foundation -supported projects is the development of alternative livelihood opportunities for forest dependent communities through revolving funds channeled through community based organizations (CBO). Revolving loan funds (RLF) have been established in 558 CBOs providing access to micro-finance to about 12,500 forest dependent poor people for alternative livelihood development. Since 2009, AF has given BDT 24.23 million of RLF grants to 558 groups of forest dependent people under all its projects. As of December 2013, about 4,000 poor forest dependent people received RLF loans for alternative income generating activities (vegetable cultivation, small businesses, livestock rearing, fish cultivation, handicrafts making, etc.). Through effective use of the RLF loans, the borrowers have been able to improve their economic conditions and repaid their loans on time. The rate of repayment of RLF loans is over 90%. Thorough cost-benefit analysis of the most profitable AIGA has been done in each of the project sites. Till July 2014, agricultural activities and cow rearing were identified as the two of the most profitable AIGA in the project site of ‘Proshika Kendra, Khagrachari’ and duck rearing and vegetable cultivation were pointed out as two of the most profitable AIGA in the project site of CNRS. Organizational and financial management capacities of the CBOs were raised through training and coaching so that they could continue managing the RLF, participatory savings and other funds in a sustainable manner after the end of the projects. The CBOs were encouraged and assisted to get registered with the Department of Cooperatives. About 70 CBOs already have got such registration. To enhance their organizational strength and registration, the village-based small CBOs have been federated at Union level.

    Documentation of biodiversity of different forest areas

    Biodiversity (flora, fauna) of six national protected areas (Rema-Kalenga, Dudhpukuria-Dophakhali, Fasiakhali and Teknaf Wildlife Sanctuaries, Inani National Park, Sitakunda Eco-park) and two community-conserved forests (in CHT) have been documented along with prescriptions for habitat management that would help in the development of management plans for those forests. Through this study, 493 species of plants, including 79 species of medicinal plants and 600 species of wildlife (139 invertebrates, 89 fishes, 25 amphibians, 57 reptiles, 245 birds and 45 mammals) have been identified and documented in the six PA in the eastern and south-eastern hill forests of Bangladesh. The most significant outcome of this study is that it has photographic evidence of species including some very rare species found in different forests for the first time in Bangladesh such as Particolored Flying Squirrel recorded at Rema-Kalenga WS, Hog-Badger recorded at Teknaf WS and Pallas’s squirrel recorded at Dudhpukuria-Dhopachari WS. A web-based database of flora and fauna of the protected areas of Bangladesh has been established based on the findings of these projects. The data-base (http://www.arannayk.org/biotrack_database/) enables people from all over world to access the photographs and brief descriptions of the plants and animals of the protected areas of Bangladesh. The database provides options for searching information by PA as well as by species names.

    Empowerment of women

    The AF supported projects have made significant contribution to women’s empowerment too. More than 60% of the total project participants of Arannayk’s projects are women. Women constitute 48% of the total executive committee members of the community based organizations of forest dependent people organized by the Arannayk projects. Due to capacity building support, these women are now able to perform their decision making as well as management roles in their own CBOs. They also have strengthened their position in the society.

    Sustainability of Project Interventions

    The Arannayk Foundation develops CBOs and build their capacities for organizational development as well as technical capacity development. It also helps in establishing a revolving loan fund, with contributions from the community members and matching grant from the AF project. To ensure sustainability of interventions, the AF helps in establishing linkages with other financial institutions, Govt. extension departments as well as Cooperative Department so that they are monitored and have access to larger finance, if any member of the CBOs needed.
